# Such a Thing as Society
## On the cost of neglect as economic policy

---

There's an old cartoon - a rowboat, water gushing through a hole in one end, and two men in the dry end, arms folded, perfectly serene: "Glad the hole isn't in our end."

It's a good joke. It's a better policy description. For fifty years it's been the operating philosophy of the wealthiest nation in human history.

In 2021, Congress expanded the Child Tax Credit - a monthly payment to families with children, no strings, no means-testing theater, just money where the water was rising. Child poverty fell to 5.2%, the lowest rate ever recorded in the history of recording it.[1] In 2022, Congress let it lapse. Child poverty doubled to 12.4% in a single year - the largest single-year increase in over fifty years of measurement - and 5.2 million children crossed the poverty line going down and then crossed it again coming back up, in the time it took a provision to expire.

Not a recession. Not a war. Not a structural shift in the economy. A subtraction. One factor pulled from the product.

And that word; *product*. Not decoration. Multiplication: factors, each one load-bearing, where a single zero bankrupts everything it touches. It's also the word for what a society produces. Those turn out to be the same sentence.

• • •

I've been working on a mathematical result - proven axiomatically, validated across eight empirical domains - showing that when independent dimensions combine to produce an emergent outcome, the composition is multiplicative.[2] Not a sum. A product. The proof follows from five constraints that are genuinely hard to argue with: if any dimension's absent, the outcome's zero; the function's continuous and monotonic; it's scale-consistent; the dimensions don't smuggle in hidden interaction terms. Given those, the multiplicative form is the only valid composition function. There's no additive alternative. The math looked for one. It isn't there.

And what falls out of the calculus should keep policymakers up at night: the marginal return on investment in any dimension scales inversely with that dimension's current value. The lower the dimension, the steeper the return. Not as moral intuition - as a derivative. When x is near zero, the slope's nearly vertical. When x is already large, the slope's a plateau pretending to be a hill.

The returns are at the bottom. They've always been at the bottom.

And if any single dimension hits zero, the product's zero. No compensation from the other dimensions, no partial credit, no grading on a curve. The system doesn't degrade gracefully - it drops off a cliff. One factor at zero, and the rest of the equation is decorative.

• • •

Watch what this looks like in one human life.

Someone without stable housing - living the impossible arithmetic of shelters and sidewalks - gets offered job training. If capability composes multiplicatively from housing × healthcare × education × financial security × social connection, then a person whose housing dimension is zero gets zero return from that training. Not diminished returns. Zero. Because they can't sleep, can't shower, can't hold an address, can't store the documents the program requires, can't keep the schedule the instructor demands, can't show up consistently enough for the investment to attach to anything - and everything the system pours in multiplies against that zero and evaporates. It isn't waste in the usual bureaucratic sense. It's colder than waste. It's investment that was structurally guaranteed to fail before the first dollar moved.

The conventional model hands out better buckets - job training here, a clinic visit there, a food bank on Thursdays - and measures the bailing in gallons per hour while the hole does what holes do. At national scale, gallons per hour is called GDP.

Housing First programs found the alternative by accident, then proved it with data that leaves no interpretive room. The CDC reviewed 26 studies: every dollar invested returns $1.44 in downstream savings.[3] Canada ran a randomized controlled trial - five cities, 950 participants - and got 73% stably housed at one year against 31% for treatment-as-usual, with 69% of the cost offset by the emergency infrastructure nobody needed anymore.[4] Finland made it national policy in 2008 and long-term homelessness fell 68% over fourteen years, which makes Finland the only country in the European Union where homelessness has declined.[5]

The only one.

Housing First doesn't work because housing is the most important dimension. It works because housing was the zero. Until you repair the zero, nothing else compounds - the piecemeal model keeps feeding resources into the nonzero dimensions while the zero swallows the product whole.

And the bootstrap narrative - work harder, want it more, pull yourself up - carries a joke inside it that everyone forgot. "Pull yourself up by your bootstraps" entered the language as satire, a physics gag, a description of something impossible.[6] The instruction manual for escaping poverty is a punchline that forgot it was joking. The math agrees with the original joke: effort in the nonzero dimensions multiplies by zero. There's no bootstrap exception in the calculus. There's no grit coefficient. Neither the equations nor the data have one.

• • •

Now scale it up.

If a nation's resilience composes multiplicatively from safety nets × healthcare × employment × infrastructure × institutional trust, then in a crisis, the dimensions nearest zero pay the steepest returns per dollar - not because compassion says so but because the derivative does, and the derivative doesn't care whether you're a Keynesian or an Austrian or a podcast libertarian. It just tells you where the slope is.

Austerity inverts this with surgical precision. It cuts social programs, unemployment support, and healthcare - the dimensions nearest zero, where the slope is a wall - while protecting bank recapitalization, corporate liquidity, and bond market confidence, the dimensions already comfortably above zero, where the slope is a floor. In multiplicative terms, austerity invests where returns are lowest and divests where they're highest. It is, by construction, the least efficient allocation of crisis resources a government could design if it were trying.

Greece and Iceland ran the controlled experiment. Same crash, 2008, same ocean of collapsing credit. Greece chose austerity: GDP down 26% in five years, unemployment peaking at 28%, six in ten young Greeks with nowhere to be on a Monday morning, and the debt-to-GDP ratio - the single metric austerity was invented to fix - climbing from 130% to 180%.[7] The medicine caused a worse version of the disease.

Iceland went the other way: let the banks fail, shield domestic deposits, protect the floor, and prosecute the bankers - twenty-nine of them went to prison. GDP recovered by 2015, unemployment fell to 3%, IMF loans repaid ahead of schedule.[8] Same crisis. Opposite policy. Opposite result. The end that protected its floor recovered. The end that cut it didn't.

The US and UK replicated it a year later as if on assignment. The US passed the Recovery Act - $836 billion aimed at the lowest dimensions: unemployment compensation, state fiscal relief, infrastructure, food assistance - and the CBO credits it with up to 4.1% of GDP and 2.8 million jobs.[9] The UK chose austerity under Cameron and Osborne, and what followed reads like a demolition schedule for a social contract: capital spending down 32% in real terms, food bank centres multiplying from 35 to nearly 1,300, emergency food parcels from 60,000 to 2.89 million, homelessness up 34%, life expectancy - the number that only ever moves in one direction in a functioning country - flat. After fourteen years of cutting, government debt rose from 65% to 98% of GDP.[10]

Fourteen years of sacrifice to achieve the exact opposite of the stated objective. Mission accomplished.

Even the IMF - not historically a hotbed of redistribution - published the correction: the actual fiscal multiplier was 0.9 to 1.7, roughly double the 0.5 their models had assumed.[11] Cutting the lowest dimensions cost twice what every forecaster's spreadsheet predicted. The models were wrong because the models assumed the world adds. It multiplies.

• • •

Scale up once more and the math turns genuinely frightening.

Venezuela sits on 303 billion barrels of proven oil - roughly 18% of the planet's reserves, more than Saudi Arabia - plus farmland, infrastructure, an educated workforce.[12] None of it mattered. GDP peaked at $372.6 billion in 2012 and by 2020 had fallen 88% - deeper than the Great Depression, worse than Syria mid-war - while per capita income collapsed from $12,607 to $1,509. The oil never left. The reserves never shrank. What sank was the one dimension you can't drill for: institutional trust, the World Bank's Rule of Law measure falling from -0.76 to -2.35, the corruption index bottoming at 10 out of 100.[13] One dimension went to zero and three hundred billion barrels of oil became ballast. The collapse didn't check tickets - the dry end drowned in first class.

Zimbabwe told the same story with soil instead of crude: productive farmland, an educated workforce, functioning infrastructure, all of it multiplied against zero governance after the land seizures began in 2000. Food production down 60% in a decade. Hyperinflation peaking at 79.6 billion percent a month - prices doubling every twenty-four hours, a currency dying in real time. Per capita income fell to a level last seen in 1952.[14]

Do the arithmetic. In both countries, the big dimensions survived - the oil, the farmland, the human capital, the physical plant. One dimension collapsed. An additive model says losing one of four dimensions costs you about a quarter of your output. The actual losses ran 78 to 90 percent. That gap - between a quarter and nine-tenths - is the distance between addition and multiplication. That's the cliff. That's what a product does when one factor floods.

• • •

Which brings us to the conventional wisdom, and where it breaks.

For forty years the dominant policy framework across the West has been: pour at the top. Cut taxes on wealth and corporations and the benefits will find their way down - a rising tide lifts all boats, greed is good, the whole cinematic catechism of the additive model. Boost the top, the gains distribute, everyone's sum increases. It's a clean story. It's also wrong, and it's no longer a matter of opinion.

The multiplicative result says trickle-down invests precisely where the derivative is flattest. When x is already enormous, the slope is horizontal - you're pouring water onto the dry end of the boat and waiting for it to run uphill. It doesn't. The returns at the top are, within measurement error, zero.

And the record is not ambiguous. Hope and Limberg examined fifty years of major tax cuts for the rich across eighteen OECD countries - 1965 to 2015 - and found no statistically significant effect on growth or employment. None. Across five decades and eighteen national economies, the only measurable outcome was income concentration at the top.[15] The Congressional Research Service ran sixty-five years of top-rate reductions and found no association with saving, investment, or productivity - the only thing the cuts tracked was the top 0.1% share climbing from 4.2% to 12.3% - and that report was withdrawn under Senate Republican pressure and then reissued, substantively unchanged, because math is difficult to lobby.[16] The IMF concluded the equity-efficiency trade-off is "largely illusory."[17] Kansas ran what its own governor called a "real live experiment" - top rate slashed 30%, pass-through income taxed at zero - and grew at half the national rate, so conclusively that a Republican legislature repealed the cuts over the governor's veto.[18]

Now the other end of the boat. In rural Kenya, unconditional transfers of about $1,000 to 10,500 poor households produced a local fiscal multiplier of 2.5 - every dollar at the bottom generating $2.50 in economic activity, with spillovers to non-recipients and minimal inflation.[19] The BRAC graduation program pushed multiple dimensions simultaneously - assets, food support, training, coaching, savings, health - across six countries in randomized trials and returned 133% to 433%, with the multidimensional package beating single-component interventions inside the same study. The multiplicative approach against the additive one, head to head, under randomization.[20] Chetty, Hendren, and Katz found children who moved to lower-poverty neighborhoods before thirteen earned 31% more as adults - and the returns were largest for those who started lowest.[21]

The steepest part of the curve. Every time.

Set them side by side. Fifty years of pouring at the top: nothing measurable, eighteen countries, zero data points in the theory's own defense. Six randomized trials at the bottom: 2.5 to 4.3 times the investment. One allocation is provably suboptimal. The other is provably optimal. The math closed the question.

What stays open is why we keep pouring on the dry end.

• • •

On July 4, 2025 - under actual fireworks, timing no fiction editor would allow - the United States signed the One Big Beautiful Bill Act. It cuts roughly $1 trillion from Medicaid and $186 billion from SNAP over ten years - the two largest safety-net programs in the country, serving the lowest dimensions of the most vulnerable people in it - while making the 2017 tax cuts permanent, raising the estate-tax exemption to $15 million, and restoring full bonus depreciation for corporations.[22] The CBO's distributional analysis is almost clinical: the bottom decile loses 3.1% of its resources; the top decile gains 2.7%.

The floor gets cut. The ceiling gets reupholstered. The gap doesn't widen as a side effect - widening the gap *is* the mechanism, even if "the Dow is over 50,000."

The hole indicators aren't projections. They're in the water now. Homelessness hit 771,480 in HUD's 2024 count - up 18%, the largest jump since counting began.[23] In November 2025, SNAP funding lapsed for the first time in the program's history - 42 million people, food assistance gone, are there no workhouses - and the food banks hit record demand.[24] Curtis Medical Center in Nebraska became the first rural hospital to close specifically because of anticipated Medicaid cuts. More than 300 others are in line behind it.[25]

And institutional trust - the dimension that in Venezuela and Zimbabwe swallowed the oil and the farmland and the educated workforce and everything else attached to them - is moving. V-Dem downgraded the United States from "liberal democracy" to "electoral democracy" for the first time in over fifty years.[26] Freedom House scored it the lowest since the current system began.[27] The Economist Intelligence Unit recorded its lowest American score since the index existed.[28] Gallup has trust in the federal government near five-decade lows.[29]

None of that is collapse. But multiplication doesn't need collapse. It needs one zero, and it can wait. Multiplication is patient.

• • •

The math doesn't have a political opinion. It has a structure, and the structure says the returns are at the bottom - not because the bottom deserves them, not because mercy demands them, but because that's where the curve goes vertical. Helping the most vulnerable isn't charity. It's the highest-yield investment available to the people doing the helping. Self-interest and mercy turn out to be the same line item. They were never in tension. We were handed the wrong model.

So yeah - fifty years of the additive story: pour at the top, the gains distribute. The multiplicative result says the story is structurally false, and the evidence - eighteen countries, six randomized trials, two failed states, two natural experiments in crisis response - says exactly what the math says. Zero at the top. Two-and-a-half to four times at the bottom. It was never a values question. It was a math question, and it's been answered.

Why we keep pouring on the dry end is a different kind of question - one the calculus can't reach but the donor rolls answer plainly enough. A system that profits from the wrong allocation has structural reasons to keep it, and structural machinery to make sure the people bailing blame each other instead of the hole. That's not mathematics. That's politics. I'll leave it there.

Thatcher said there's no such thing as society - only individual men and women, only families. Passengers, in other words. Seats.

But here's what the cartoon knows that fifty years of policy never learned: the plug isn't stored anywhere. Neither end owns it. It doesn't exist until the boat acts like a boat - until the dry end climbs over the thwart, not out of kindness but because the math finally told it what the water always knew. Society isn't the boat.

Society is the plug.

It's a product, not a sum. It's only as strong as its weakest factor, and one flooded factor takes the whole equation down regardless of how magnificent the other terms are. The returns are at the bottom. They always were. The dry end spent fifty years filing them under *compassion* - and teaching everyone else the word meant weakness - so no one would ever come to collect them.

They've been there the whole time.
