# By Any Means
## On subjective opportunity and objective cost

---

On the night of November 14, 1917, roughly forty guards descended on thirty-three women at the Occoquan Workhouse in Virginia. Their crime was picketing. They had stood at the White House gates holding signs that asked why a president waging war to make the world safe for democracy would not extend democracy to half his own citizens. For this they were arrested, imprisoned, and - on that night - beaten.

Lucy Burns was handcuffed to the bars of her cell with her arms above her head and left hanging there until morning. Dora Lewis was thrown headfirst into a dark cell and knocked unconscious against the iron frame of a bed. Alice Cosu, watching Lewis crumple and believing her dead, suffered a heart attack. The guards denied her medical care until dawn. Dorothy Day - who would later found the Catholic Worker movement - was slammed twice over the back of an iron bench. [1] The superintendent who ordered it was never prosecuted. The women who survived it went on hunger strikes, so the state held them down and forced tubes into their throats. And within three years, the Nineteenth Amendment was ratified.

The right to vote was won by people who could not vote, through methods that were not voting.

This is not an exceptional case. It is the pattern.

---

### I.

A woman refused to give up her seat on a segregated bus in Montgomery, Alabama. She was handcuffed, dragged off by police, and jailed. She'd been studying Black history in school, reading about the constitution. She knew exactly what she was doing.

Her name was Claudette Colvin. She was fifteen years old. It was March 1955. [2]

Nine months later, Rosa Parks did the same thing on the same bus system and became the icon. Colvin didn't. Too dark-skinned. Got pregnant at sixteen. Movement leaders called her "mouthy" and "emotional." Parks was the better casting. "Stolid, calm, unflappable." Forty-two. A seamstress. The right face for the poster.

Even liberation has a casting call.

And the woman they did cast? Also not what they told you. Rosa Parks was not a tired seamstress who spontaneously decided she'd had enough. She was the elected secretary of the Montgomery NAACP, trained in civil disobedience at the Highlander Folk School. The boycott wasn't spontaneous either. The Women's Political Council had the carpool network built before Parks ever sat down. Their president, Jo Ann Gibson Robinson, said it plainly: "We knew that in a matter of hours, we could corral the whole city." [3]

The story you learned was wrong twice. Once about who did it first. Again about how it actually worked.

And that second error is the one that matters, because the textbook version does something specific. It takes a planned campaign of economic warfare and flattens it into a moment of personal courage. It erases the organization, the strategy, the discipline, the 381 days of walking to work through Alabama heat. It makes change look like something that happens to you. Not something you build.

That's not a gap in the curriculum. That's a feature of the show.

---

### II.

Here is what the show cuts.

The Montgomery boycott ran 381 days and the bus company bled 65 to 68 percent of its revenue - roughly three thousand dollars a day, gone, every day, for over a year. [4] The Greensboro sit-ins cost Woolworth's more than $200,000; the store manager's own salary was cut for poor performance before the lunch counter quietly desegregated. [5] The Birmingham boycott cut downtown business 40 percent, and merchant Isadore Pizitz eventually said the quiet part into a microphone: "It took the spur of the economic boycott to move us in the right direction." [6] No textbook frames that sentence as what it is. A confession.

The mechanism was never moral suasion. It was cost imposition. The system conceded not because it was persuaded but because the books stopped balancing.

Run the ledger anywhere you like and it reads the same. Gandhi's boycott of British cloth cut imports nearly in half in a single year, shuttered seventy-four textile mills in Blackburn, and threw 160,000 Lancashire workers into the street - his campaign didn't appeal to British morality, it destroyed British jobs, and when he toured the dead mills in 1931 the only question constituents were putting to their members of Parliament, preserved in Hansard, was: "When are you going to get our cotton mills to work again?" [7] The eight-hour workday took 105 years of strikes and blood. Haymarket, where four anarchists were hanged for a bomb none of them was proven to have thrown. Ludlow, where the National Guard machine-gunned a tent colony and two women and eleven children suffocated and burned under a single canvas. Triangle Shirtwaist, where 146 workers - young immigrant women, some fourteen years old - died behind doors their employer had locked to prevent theft. [8] The legislation followed the bodies. It always does.

Abolition took a civil war and 620,000 dead; the ballot box, left to itself, had produced the Fugitive Slave Act and Dred Scott. Apartheid fell after decades of strikes, armed resistance, and sanctions that chased 122 American multinationals out of South Africa in two years - the 1994 election was the result of apartheid ending, not the cause. [9] American independence was armed treason by men who would have hanged for it.

Every one of these gets taught as a triumph of the democratic spirit. Not one of them was democratic. Every single one required operating outside the system - the strikes, the boycotts, the bodies in the street. The one function that actually produced every major change in civilization is the one the story scrubs out.

Shows don't close because the critics hate them. They close when the house is empty.

---

### III.

Read the record as a dataset instead of a shelf of inspirational stories and three facts fall out of it.

First: in every case, the people who needed the change were locked out of the mechanism that was supposed to deliver it. Enslaved people could not vote to end slavery. Women could not vote themselves suffrage. You cannot vote your way into the right to vote. The sentence chases its own tail. That was the design.

Second: even getting the right didn't secure it. The Fifteenth Amendment put Black suffrage on paper in 1870, and the architecture spent the next ninety-five years unwriting it - poll taxes, literacy tests, grandfather clauses, white primaries, the Klan. Half a million Black men voted during Reconstruction. Nearly two thousand won public office. Then the federal troops went home, and the system adapted, and it kept adapting for ninety-five years until bodies on the Edmund Pettus Bridge made the cost of nullification higher than the cost of the franchise. [10]

Third: the formal legal change followed the disruption. Every time. The Thirteenth Amendment followed the war. The Nineteenth followed the Night of Terror. The Civil Rights Act followed Birmingham. The Fair Labor Standards Act followed a century of strikes. The ballot formalized what bodies in the street had already forced. The movement created the conditions. The vote was the notarization.

And in every instance - every single one - the people who said *wait* were wrong.

---

### IV.

That's the history. Here's why the pattern holds now.

Both parties drink from the same financial well. Three of the last five Treasury Secretaries came from Goldman Sachs. Robert Rubin went from Goldman to Treasury, pushed through the repeal of Glass-Steagall - the law that had prevented major bank failures for sixty-six straight years - then walked into Citigroup, the company his own deregulation had just made legal, and collected $126 million. The repeal passed the Senate 90 to 8. Bipartisan. The crisis it enabled destroyed eleven trillion dollars in household wealth. [11] Nobody involved was voted into that sequence and nobody could be voted out of it, because both names on the ballot answered to the same donors.

The PATRIOT Act: 342 pages, three days, passed 98 to 1 by members openly admitting they hadn't read it. [12] TARP: both parties. The $886 billion defense budget: all thirteen senators holding defense contractor stock voted yes - one hundred percent alignment between portfolio and vote. [13] Representative Patrick Fallon held Boeing stock while questioning officials about the V-22 Osprey crashes that killed twenty airmen, then shook hands with the victims' families on his way out of the hearing. [14]

None of this is hidden. It's disclosed, filed, published, and legal. That's the tell. A conspiracy hides. An architecture doesn't have to.

The culture war is real - the differences on abortion, guns, immigration are genuine and they matter to millions of people. But look at which differences the money tolerates. The donor class can live with either outcome on abortion. It cannot live with progressive taxation. It can absorb any direction on immigration. It cannot absorb the carried interest loophole closing. The fights you're allowed to have are the fights that cost them nothing. The culture war is what's on the screen. The extraction is the business office.

You pick your screen. You pick your seat. You think you chose. But every screen is in the same building, and the owner decided what was playing before the doors opened.

---

### V.

The strongest counterargument is the New Deal. FDR was elected, and the ballot appeared to deliver: progressive taxation, Social Security, the Great Compression - top marginal rates above 90 percent, incomes doubling at every percentile, the largest middle class in human history. Voting, transforming everything.

Except that's not what happened. The New Deal wasn't the show changing. It was an intermission.

It arrived as the system's emergency response to a legitimacy crisis built by disruption - the Depression, general strikes, and seventeen thousand Bonus Army veterans camped in Washington until tanks rolled over them and their shantytown burned on the front pages. FDR looked at the photographs of MacArthur's cavalry charging American veterans and said, "this will elect me." [15] It did. The system then conceded the minimum required to survive.

And the concession came invoiced. FDR got his economic reforms. J. Edgar Hoover got the FBI.

The reforms compressed incomes for forty years. The bureau ran for forty-eight - surveilling, infiltrating, and dismantling every movement that might demand the next concession. COINTELPRO's files, when the Church Committee finally pried them open, included an anonymous letter the FBI mailed to Martin Luther King Jr. alongside tapes of his private life, telling him there was "only one thing left for you to do." [16] They meant his own death, before the Nobel ceremony.

So take the counterargument at full strength. The question is not whether the system can produce a New Deal. It can, under duress. The question is what it builds alongside one, and which of the two lasts longer.

---

### VI.

Because the system doesn't just concede. It studies.

The Voting Rights Act of 1965 worked - registration soared, the ledger of the Edmund Pettus Bridge got paid. So the architecture went back to school. In 2013, Shelby County v. Holder gutted the Act's preclearance formula, with the Chief Justice explaining that "things have changed dramatically" in the South. [17] Texas announced its voter ID law the same day. Within a few years, 1,688 polling places had closed across previously covered states, and a federal court found North Carolina had targeted Black voters "with almost surgical precision." [18] In 2026, Louisiana v. Callais finished the job on Section 2 - Justice Kagan's dissent called the provision "all but a dead letter," in a case decided after more than 100,000 Louisianans had already cast ballots in a primary the ruling suspended. [19] Citizens United runs the same play on the other flank: money is speech, corporations are speakers, and the ceiling on what an oligarch may spend on an election is gone. [20]

Nobody repealed the Fifteenth Amendment. Nobody had to. The franchise still exists. It is now surrounded.

---

### VII.

Which brings us to the part of the ledger with your name on it.

The largest empirical study of American policy ever run - 1,779 policy outcomes over twenty years - found that the preferences of average citizens have a "near-zero, statistically non-significant impact" on what becomes law. [21] Near-zero. That's a physics term. It describes the gravitational pull of your opinion. Bartels found senators' votes show "no apparent effect" from the bottom third of their constituents by income - not a small effect, none. [22] Eighty-six percent of Americans, across both parties, want members of Congress banned from trading stocks. The number of bills passed is zero. [23] Insulin that costs a few dollars to make sells at markups past 5,000 percent while pharmacy benefit managers pocket 99.3 percent of certain rebate streams, and the men who set those prices testify before committees whose members hold their stock. [24]

Lyndon Johnson had a name for the people the government actually answers to. He called them "the interest groups." He wasn't warning anybody. He was describing his customer base.

None of this says your ballot is worthless. It says your ballot is priced. And the market has told you, in twenty years of data, exactly what it's worth.

---

### VIII.

Here's the part they really don't want in the curriculum: none of this is a betrayal of the American tradition. It *is* the American tradition.

Thoreau, 1849: "Even voting for the right is doing nothing for it." [25] Frederick Douglass, 1867, listed a man's rights as resting in three boxes - the ballot box, the jury box, and the cartridge box - and he ordered them deliberately. [26] King, from a Birmingham jail cell, on the counsel of patience: "This 'Wait' has almost always meant 'Never.'" [27] And underneath all of them, the founding document itself - the one framed in every classroom - declaring that when a government becomes destructive of its ends, "it is their right, it is their duty, to throw off such Government." [28]

The founders committed treason and called it a declaration. The suffragists broke the law and were beaten for it in a Virginia workhouse. The civil rights movement violated segregation statutes as strategy, on purpose, in public. Every generation that expanded American freedom did it by operating outside rules that were themselves the problem - and then the next generation's textbooks filed the rule-breaking under patriotism and taught the children that the system worked.

The disobedience is not the deviation. The disobedience is the tradition. The obedience is the innovation.

---

### IX.

The ballot box exists. It has always existed. It has never, by itself, been enough.

A ticket is a real thing. This one cost real blood to print - women hung from cell bars in Virginia so their granddaughters could hold it, and that is not nothing. That is admission. The ticket gets you into the building. It lets you pick a screen and take a seat. It has never once decided what's playing.

Every audience that ever changed the program did it the same way: from the street, not the seat - by making the show cost more than the concession.

So vote. Take the ticket. It's paid for. Just don't mistake it for the deed to the theater.

The question is not whether you should vote. The question is whether you believe the ticket is the whole building - and what three hundred years of evidence say about that belief.
