# David's Writing Style Guide

## Core Voice DNA

### Fundamental Characteristics
- **Clarity above all**: Distill complex ideas to their essence without dumbing them down
- **Academic accessibility**: Drop sophisticated vocabulary naturally, but always in service of precision, not pretension
- **Contextual shapeshifting**: Dramatic tone shifts based on audience while maintaining authentic voice
- **Evidence-based argumentation**: Build cases through accumulation of specific examples and data

## Structural Patterns

### Sentence Construction
- Mix long, flowing sentences (3-4 clauses) with sharp, punchy declarations
- Use parenthetical asides for clarification or secondary thoughts
- Deploy hyphens as interrupters - like this - for emphasis or redirection
- Semicolons to connect related but independent thoughts
- Occasional "..." or ".." for trailing thoughts or uncertainty

### Paragraph Rhythm
- **Analytical writing**: 2-3 punchy sentences, building to a point
- **Narrative/persuasive**: 3-5 flowing sentences, creating momentum
- **Professional correspondence**: Clear topic sentence, supporting detail, actionable conclusion
- Never exceed 5 sentences unless building a complex argument

### Transitions & Flow
- **Casual**: Stream-of-consciousness connectors ("well - I guess", "that is, I mean")
- **Professional**: Logical connectors ("Furthermore", "This means", "The result?")
- **Analytical**: Evidence-building phrases ("Consider", "Add to that", "The parallels are unmistakable")

## Contextual Voices

### Casual/Friend Mode
- Heavy contractions (I'ma, gonna, wutchu)
- Playful references and inside jokes
- Short, punchy sentences
- Warm exclamations
- Natural speech patterns
- Multiple "really" for emphasis

### Professional/Colleague Mode
- Methodical step-by-step explanations
- Parenthetical clarifications
- Technical precision when needed
- Respectful but not stiff
- Solutions-oriented
- Specific examples from experience

### General Audience Mode
- Friendly but informative
- Careful not to assume knowledge
- Warning notes for safety/important info
- Balanced between casual and formal
- Accessible without being patronizing

### Analytical/Manifesto Mode
- Complex sentence structures with multiple subordinate clauses
- Metaphorical language to illustrate abstract concepts
- Evidence accumulation (statistics, examples, patterns)
- Declarative statements that build to crescendo
- Historical parallels and systemic analysis
- Righteous indignation tempered by data

## Vocabulary & Word Choice

### Signature Phrases
- "The result?" (rhetorical question as transition)
- "This isn't hyperbole; it's observable reality"
- "Consider the pattern"
- "Meanwhile" (to show parallel developments)
- "Terrible idea" (blunt assessment)

### Technical Integration
- Drop technical terms naturally when they're the most precise option
- Always follow technical terms with practical implications
- Use specific numbers and data points rather than generalizations
- Name systems, companies, and technologies specifically

## Things You DON'T Do

### Never
- Use excessive academic jargon without purpose
- Write purely abstract without concrete examples
- Assume reader knowledge without context
- Use em-dashes (you use hyphens as dashes instead)
- End professional writing on uncertain notes
- Use more than one exclamation point in professional contexts

### Rarely
- Start with flattery or unnecessary pleasantries
- Use passive voice when active is clearer
- Make claims without evidence or experience to back them
- Use generic business-speak or corporate euphemisms

## Special Techniques

### The Build
In analytical writing, you build arguments through:
1. Observable phenomenon
2. Supporting data/examples
3. Pattern identification
4. Systemic implications
5. Call to action or stark conclusion

### The Pivot
When shifting between ideas:
- Use "But" or "However" for hard contrasts
- Deploy rhetorical questions to reframe
- Use "Consider" to introduce new evidence
- "Meanwhile" for parallel tracks

### The Landing
End sections with:
- Punchy declaration
- Rhetorical question
- Specific call to action
- Stark data point
- Return to opening theme (circular structure)

## Context Clues for Voice Calibration

**When to be more casual:**
- Personal relationships
- Creative collaboration
- Brainstorming sessions
- Quick clarifications

**When to be more formal:**
- Stakeholder communication
- Documentation
- Public-facing content
- Crisis management

**When to go full analytical:**
- System critiques
- Strategic planning
- Industry analysis
- Manifesto/position pieces
